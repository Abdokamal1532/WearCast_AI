━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PROBLEM #1 — MASK BLEEDS INTO BACKGROUND (CRITICAL — THE MAIN CAUSE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: utils_wearcast.py → get_mask_location() → add_arm_mask_local()
         inference_wearcast_hd.py → get_mask_location() → spatial_prior

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  The arm mask generation uses a convex hull based on shoulder + elbow keypoints
  with ARM_PAD = 35px. This ARM_PAD value is too large and causes the arm region
  to extend OUTSIDE the body boundary into the background.

  The body_mask_dilated constraint that tries to prevent background bleed uses a
  9×9 dilation which adds ~4-5px of expansion — far too small to counteract the
  35px arm padding. Result: the mask bleeds into the background.

  In inference_wearcast_hd.py, the spatial_prior horizontal corridor uses
  s_width * 0.65 + 15px padding, which for a normal person (shoulder width ~180px)
  gives corridor_half = 132px. This is wider than the actual shirt, so garment
  edges bleed outside the torso into background pixels.

  THE DAMAGE: The UNet sees background pixels inside the mask, gets confused, and
  generates the garment overlapping/floating over the background → "cape effect".

PROBLEM #2 — MASK SIZE MISMATCH (CRITICAL)
────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: inference_wearcast_hd.py line ~243
         mask = mask.resize((768, 1024), Image.NEAREST)

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  The mask is generated at 384×512 (because model_parse is 384×512) but is then
  resized to 768×1024 using Image.NEAREST. NEAREST neighbor upscaling creates a
  "blocky" mask with harsh pixelated edges at 2× scale. This means the feathering
  done BEFORE the resize is wasted — the resize re-introduces hard block edges.

  THE DAMAGE: Visible hard boundary seams around the garment edges in the output.
  The garment looks "pasted on" because the edge is pixel-jagged.

PROBLEM #3 — MASK GENERATION RUNS AT 384×512 BUT IMAGE IS 768×1024 (CRITICAL)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: inference_wearcast_hd.py → self.get_mask_location()

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  Human parsing (model_parse) returns a 384×512 segmentation map. All the
  careful geometric calculations — arm paddings, shoulder widths, neck boxes,
  spatial priors — are computed at 384×512 scale. Then the mask is upscaled 2×.

  A 35px ARM_PAD at 384×512 scale becomes effectively 70px at 768×1024 scale.
  So even if 35px was correct at low resolution, it becomes doubly wrong at
  the actual inference resolution.

  THE DAMAGE: Everything is off by a factor of 2 in pixel coordinates.

PROBLEM #4 — COMPOSITE MASK IS NOT TIGHT ENOUGH TO BODY (HIGH)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: inference_wearcast_hd.py lines 426–433

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  The post-processing compositing uses erode(3×3, 1 iter) + GaussianBlur(sigma=5).
  The erosion is too small (3×3, only 1px effective shrink at each side). The
  feather sigma=5.0 creates a wide soft edge that bleeds the generated garment
  pixels into the background by ~15px on each side.

  For large images (768×1024), a sigma=5 creates almost invisible feathering —
  it's too sharp for the shirt collar/sleeve boundary but too soft for the torso
  edge, giving the transparent ghostly blending look.

  THE DAMAGE: The "ghost" semi-transparent look in the final output. The outer
  edges of the shirt fade from the new garment → background instead of stopping
  cleanly at the body silhouette.

PROBLEM #5 — LUMINANCE CORRECTION IS FIGHTING THE UNet (MEDIUM)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: inference_wearcast_hd.py lines 456–461

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  The luminance shift formula: shift = clip(lum_gap * 0.6, -50, 50)
  When the UNet generates a dark garment (correct, since the t-shirt IS dark)
  and the garment reference is also dark, the gap should be near 0. But because
  the mask includes background pixels (see Problem #1), the "gen_in_mask" mean
  luminance includes bright background pixels. This inflates lum_gap and causes
  an overcorrection that lightens the dark shirt, making it look grey/washed out.

  THE DAMAGE: The generated black shirt is shifted to appear grey/transparent
  instead of staying black, contributing to the "ghost garment" look.

PROBLEM #6 — NECK MASK USES NON-EXISTENT LABEL 18 (MEDIUM)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: inference_wearcast_hd.py line 1059
         neck_mask = (parse_array == 18).astype(np.float32)

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  The LIP (Look Into Person) human parser used in OOTD uses 20 classes (0–19).
  Label 18 IS defined as "Neck(refined)" in some versions but is ABSENT in many
  Kaggle/HuggingFace deployments. When it is absent, neck_mask is all zeros and
  the neck-collar region gets NO mask coverage.

  THE DAMAGE: The collar of the new shirt is not inpainted — it either shows the
  original shirt's collar or creates a visible seam at the neck boundary.

PROBLEM #7 — image_scale=2.0 IS TOO HIGH FOR THE BASE MODEL (LOW-MEDIUM)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHERE: run_wearcast.py line 48
         image_scale=2.0

  WHAT IS HAPPENING:
  OOTDiffusion (the base model for WearCast) was trained and evaluated with
  image_guidance_scale values of 1.0–2.0. A scale of 2.0 pushes the classifier-
  free guidance very hard toward the garment reference image. This means the model
  over-copies the garment's texture/shape regardless of body pose, causing the
  garment to appear "floating" in its reference pose rather than deforming to the
  body.

  THE DAMAGE: The garment looks rigidly placed (like a product photo overlaid)
  rather than naturally draping on the person's body. Contributes to the "cape"
  floating look when combined with the mask bleed problem.

FIX #4 [CRITICAL] — Fix Post-Processing Compositing (Kill the Ghost)
FILE: inference_wearcast_hd.py → __call__() → Phase 4 compositing

CURRENT CODE (lines ~420-432):

    eroded_mask = cv2.erode(binary_mask, kernel_erode, iterations=1)

    # Step 1: Erode mask inward to guarantee no garment bleeds outside body
    kernel_erode = np.ones((5, 5), np.uint8)
    eroded_mask = cv2.erode(binary_mask, kernel_erode, iterations=2)  # 2x for tighter fit

    # Step 2: Use a HARD center + soft edge mask (not fully Gaussian)
    # This keeps the CENTER of the garment at full 1.0 alpha (no ghost)

fabric meets skin) should have soft blending. The core_mask forces center alpha
to 1.0, making the garment look solid and real. Only the edge pixels blend.

SECTION 4 — BONUS RECOMMENDATIONS FOR PHOTOREALISTIC "REAL CLOTHES CHANGE" LOOK
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RECOMMENDATION A — Add Lighting Consistency (Shadow Matching)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  The generated garment ignores the scene lighting. In the sample image, the
  lighting comes from slightly above-left. The new shirt should have shadows on
  the right side matching this.

  HOW TO IMPLEMENT:
  After compositing, apply a very subtle directional shadow overlay:

    shadow_mask = binary_mask.copy().astype(np.float32)
    # Shift shadow mask slightly right and down
    shadow_offset_x, shadow_offset_y = 8, 5  # pixels
    M = np.float32([[1, 0, shadow_offset_x], [0, 1, shadow_offset_y]])
    shadow_shifted = cv2.warpAffine(shadow_mask, M, (shadow_mask.shape[1], shadow_mask.shape[0]))
    # Shadow = area in shifted but not original = edge
    shadow_region = np.clip(shadow_shifted - shadow_mask, 0, 1)
    shadow_region = cv2.GaussianBlur(shadow_region, (0, 0), 3.0)
    # Darken slightly: reduce by 8%
    final_np[:, :, :] -= (shadow_region[:, :, np.newaxis] * 20).astype(np.float32)
    final_np = np.clip(final_np, 0, 255)

RECOMMENDATION B — Preserve Skin Color at Mask Boundary
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  At the neck and forearm boundary, the transition from skin to fabric often
  creates a color-bleed where garment color leaks onto skin or skin color leaks
  onto garment, looking unrealistic.

  HOW TO IMPLEMENT:
  Create a "skin zone" mask using the human parser (labels 11=Face, 14=LeftArm,
  15=RightArm). Wherever the compositing alpha is in range [0.1, 0.9] AND the
  original image is skin-colored, bias the blend toward the original (skin):

    skin_zone = ((parse_array == 14) | (parse_array == 15) | (parse_array == 11)).astype(np.float32)
    skin_zone_resized = cv2.resize(skin_zone, (final_np.shape[1], final_np.shape[0]))
    skin_bias = np.where((alpha > 0.1) & (alpha < 0.9) & (skin_zone_resized > 0.5), 0.7, 1.0)
    # Reduce alpha where we are near skin edges to bias toward original skin color
    alpha_skin_adjusted = alpha * skin_bias
    final_np = (gen_final * alpha_skin_adjusted[:,:,np.newaxis]) + (ori_final * (1 - alpha_skin_adjusted[:,:,np.newaxis]))

RECOMMENDATION C — Increase num_steps to 30–40 (Not 20)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  CURRENT: final_steps = max(30, ...) → but UI default is 20 and auto_params
  may be returning lower values.

  RECOMMENDATION: Always enforce minimum 30 steps, ideally 40 for HD mode.
  The reason is that OOTDiffusion's denoising quality improves significantly
  from 20→30 steps (sharper fabric texture, better color rendering) but only
  marginally from 30→40. At 20 steps, fabric textures appear slightly blurry.

  HOW TO IMPLEMENT in run_wearcast.py:
    num_steps=35  # Change from 20 to 35

  This is correct. But for the UNet's image_garm input, the white background

    if bg_coverage > 0.05:

RECOMMENDATION E — Use Poisson Blending at Boundaries (Advanced)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  For the most photorealistic result, use OpenCV's seamlessClone (Poisson
  blending) at the garment boundaries instead of simple alpha blending.
  Poisson blending ensures lighting, color, and texture gradients transition
